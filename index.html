The product

PUFGuard secures device identity without burdening the wearable.

Wearables process sensitive health, payment, and operational telemetry, yet many devices operate under strict compute, memory, and battery constraints. PUFGuard addresses this gap by using intrinsic SRAM startup behavior as an unclonable identity source and by moving policy-heavy verification to a hardened Central Authority.

01

Factory enrollment

OEM routines identify stable SRAM cells, register challenge-response material with the Central Authority, and provision each device with a minimal trust anchor.

02

Session authentication

The Central Authority issues session-unique SRAM address permutations, verifies the PUF response, and rejects replayed or inconsistent authentication attempts.

03

Secure pairing

After verification, the wearable and client complete an ephemeral key exchange and nonce-based derivation to establish a per-session AES key.

Architecture

Designed for manufacturable security and scalable fleet operations.

PUFGuard separates device enrollment, centralized verification, lightweight firmware execution, and client pairing into a production-oriented trust architecture.

1

OEM provisioning

Automated power-cycle routines identify stable SRAM cells, create CRP bundles, and upload device metadata over secure channels.

2

Central Authority

The CA stores encrypted CRPs, computes session-specific challenge permutations, verifies responses, and supports lifecycle controls.

3

Wearable firmware

Minimal firmware performs SRAM readout, error correction, ECDHE, AES-GCM, and BLE/Wi-Fi communication without storing long-term private keys.

4

Client session

Verified devices establish a fresh encrypted channel with the client for telemetry and command traffic.

Security features

Layered protection against cloning, replay, and impersonation.

PUFGuard combines a hardware-derived identity with session freshness, authenticated encryption, and lightweight error correction.

Hardware-anchored identity

Device identity is derived from stable SRAM startup behavior, making cloning substantially harder than copying a stored key.

Session-unique CRP permutation

Each authentication session uses a fresh permutation of SRAM addresses to limit replay and precomputation risk.

AES-GCM protected exchanges

PUF challenges and responses are encrypted and authenticated under ECDHE-derived symmetric keys.

Error correction for reliability

Lightweight correction logic stabilizes noisy SRAM responses under practical operating conditions.
